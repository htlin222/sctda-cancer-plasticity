\section{Introduction}

First-line osimertinib has transformed care for EGFR-mutant non-small
cell lung cancer (NSCLC). However, essentially every patient eventually
relapses from residual disease \citep{ramalingam2020,piotrowska2018}.
The residual cells, known as drug-tolerant persisters, survive targeted
therapy without acquiring resistance mutations
\citep{sharma2010,hata2016}. Early work described persisters as a
single quiescent state, but recent lineage-tracing experiments have
revealed that individual cells reversibly transition among multiple
transcriptional programs before either dying or acquiring stable
resistance \citep{oren2021,aissa2021}. This inter-state cycling is
distinct from one-directional progression along a differentiation
hierarchy and is thought to underlie the adaptive plasticity that
makes persisters so difficult to eradicate.

Standard single-cell trajectory tools, including branching-graph
methods such as Monocle and Slingshot, connectivity-graph methods
such as PAGA, and vector-field methods such as RNA velocity, were
designed to model one-directional progressions and therefore cannot
quantify how robustly cells trace a closed loop in state space
\citep{saelens2019}. When drug-tolerant cells reversibly cycle among
states, each cell is a point in gene-expression space and the
collective motion traces a loop: the same kind of closed
one-dimensional structure that distinguishes a donut from a sphere in
topology (\cref{fig:concept}a--b). Persistent homology, a branch of
computational topology, measures the robustness of such loops across
spatial scales, and the Mapper algorithm \citep{singh2007}
complements this with an interpretable graph summary. Both have been
applied to bulk cancer transcriptomes \citep{nicolau2011} and
developmental single-cell data \citep{rizvi2017}, but have not been
integrated into a reproducible framework for longitudinal
perturbation studies with formal statistical testing.

Here we present a framework that applies persistent homology and
Mapper to longitudinal single-cell RNA-seq data, with integrated
permutation-based null testing, cell-cycle controls, and Mapper-based
gene attribution. We validate the approach on five independent
EGFR-mutant lung-cancer datasets spanning two drugs (erlotinib,
osimertinib), three biological scales (cell line, patient-derived
xenograft, patient tumour), and a 14-patient longitudinal cohort.
We show that a topological measure of cyclic cell-state structure,
the maximum $H_1$ persistence, increases monotonically with drug
exposure across all systems, is not explained by cell-cycle variation
or batch effects, and is biologically anchored by enrichment of
epithelial--mesenchymal transition (EMT) programs in loop-associated
transcripts. All analyses are reproducible from raw data via a single
command under an open-source license.
