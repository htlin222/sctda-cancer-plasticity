\section{Discussion}

We have shown that cyclic topological structure in single-cell
transcriptomes increases monotonically with EGFR-TKI exposure across
five independent systems spanning cell lines, patient-derived
xenografts, and patient tumours. The primary contribution is a
reproducible, open-source framework that reframes the question from
whether drug tolerance produces topological cyclicity in any single
dataset to whether topological cyclicity is a reproducible cross-system
phenotype. Our answer is yes: max $H_1$ increases with drug exposure in
every within-system comparison, all six validation-cohort permutation
tests reach $p < 0.05$, and the signal survives cell-cycle controls and
batch correction.

Our framework complements lineage-tracing studies that established
cycling subpopulations in PC9 \citep{oren2021} and identified discrete
DTP states \citep{aissa2021} by detecting cyclic behaviour directly
from scRNA-seq without lineage-tracing infrastructure. Relative to
earlier applications of Mapper to developmental scRNA-seq
\citep{rizvi2017}, we add permutation-based null testing, cell-cycle
controls, and application to perturbation-response data. The broader
non-genetic drug-tolerance literature, including YAP-mediated
senescent persisters \citep{kurppa2020}, melanoma minimal-residual-disease
plasticity \citep{rambow2018}, and non-genetic resistance mechanisms
\citep{marine2020,shen2020}, provides biological context. Our
contribution is a quantitative, topologically grounded statistic for
detecting and comparing such structures.

An informative finding is that the topological signature replicates
across cell line and PDX with higher fidelity than the underlying gene
programs. The gene overlap between systems is highly significant but
incomplete, suggesting that plasticity is realised through partially
different repertoires depending on microenvironment, consistent with
context-dependent EMT programs \citep{dongre2019}. The topological
statistic abstracts over these specific gene choices, making it a more
stable cross-system readout than any individual gene signature.

If drug tolerance operates through topologically cyclic plasticity,
therapeutic combinations that collapse the cycle may outperform
single-state-targeting strategies. The bidirectional nature of
inter-state cycling further provides a conceptual basis for the partial
clinical efficacy of TKI rechallenge after a drug holiday
\citep{gatenby2009,enriquez-navas2016}: cells that survived prior
treatment can revert to drug-sensitive states. The cross-scale
reproducibility of the signature argues that max $H_1$ is suitable for
benchmarking drug responses across labs and may generalise to other
perturbation contexts such as immunotherapy escape or
chemotherapy-induced dedifferentiation.

Several limitations should be noted. Cross-system comparisons mix
platforms (Drop-seq and 10x Chromium); the within-system monotonic
increase is therefore the primary claim. The three matched patient
biopsies are hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory; a
prospective paired-biopsy study would be needed for within-patient
validation. Cell-cycle gene ablation removes a standard but not
exhaustive gene set; the complementary S/G2M regression control
addresses this limitation. A full cross-platform integration using
methods such as scVI \citep{lopez2018} is deferred to future work.

We speculate that cyclic plasticity reflects a Waddington-style limit
cycle around a shallow epigenetic basin \citep{huang2012,pisco2015},
making it adaptive as bet-hedging under fluctuating drug selection
\citep{brock2009,gupta2011}. Distinguishing selection of pre-existing
cycles from drug-induced de novo cycles would require lineage tracing
with controlled clonal bottlenecks. Adaptive therapy and state-trapping
combinations are testable hypotheses against the topological readout
established here.
